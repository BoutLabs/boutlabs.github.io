# ICP — Non-technical founder / business leader needing a trusted technical partner

Captured: 2026-05-19

The persona this campaign series targets. Strategist reads this when
mapping a signal to an audience; copywriter reads it when drafting
("would *this person* engage with this opening?"); brand-reviewer reads
it when checking persona match.

## Identity

- **Title / role**: Founder, CEO, COO, or VP Operations at a 50–500 person mid-market organization; OR a Director / Program Lead at a design-led nonprofit; OR a non-technical executive sponsor of a strategic technology project at an enterprise
- **Company stage / size**: Series A through mid-market PE-backed; enterprise sponsors of one-off transformation initiatives
- **Industry**: design-led nonprofit (IDEO.org adjacency), healthcare (BayCare / Moffitt adjacency), entertainment / media (Disney / THQ / Rock'n'Roll Marathons adjacency), finance / public sector (NYSE / Hillsborough County adjacency)
- **Decision authority**: can sign a 6-figure consulting engagement on their own; 7-figure needs board / CFO approval. Crucially: **can fire a vendor without an RFP** — they hire partners on trust and word-of-mouth, not procurement scorecards

## Attention

Where they actually spend time. Be specific.

- **Channels they read daily**: LinkedIn (heavy), industry-specific newsletters (Stratechery for tech-aware execs, Axios Pro Rata for founders, Chief of Staff Network for ops leaders), email digests from their existing vendors
- **Newsletters / publications**: Stratechery, Lenny's Newsletter, Not Boring (Packy McCormick), Harvard Business Review, MIT Tech Review, Modern Healthcare (for the healthcare slice)
- **Communities / forums**: small private slacks and CEO peer groups (YPO, Vistage); industry-specific CEO roundtables; alumni networks. NOT public twitter / X / reddit
- **People they follow**: their own board members; one or two operators-turned-investors; a small number of CEO peers
- **Conferences / events**: their industry's annual conference; SXSW for media-adjacent; HIMSS for healthcare; Aspen Ideas / TED-adjacent for design-led nonprofits

## Pain — what they're trying to solve

They have a technology decision in front of them that has a real downside
and an unclear upside. Examples:

- "We need a CTO but the market is brutal and we don't actually know what
  we'd hire them to do for the first 6 months."
- "Our existing platform is held together with duct tape and the team
  that built it left. We need someone to tell us if we should rebuild,
  buy, or limp."
- "We have a board mandate to ship a major digital initiative in 12 months
  and our internal IT team has never done anything like this."
- "Our agency is delivering, but I have no way to tell if what they're
  delivering is *good*. We need a fractional technical conscience."

They're not in the market for cheap, fast, or trendy. They're in the
market for *I won't get fired for hiring them*.

## Pleasure — what's working for them

- A small handful of trusted advisors they've worked with for years
- Their existing software / agency relationships — even when those relationships
  are creaky, the cost of replacing is high enough that they tolerate
- Their own pattern-matching from prior careers
- The relief of getting a *straight answer* instead of a sales pitch

## What they're tired of hearing

- "Digital transformation" as a phrase, in any context
- Anyone selling "our proprietary methodology" without showing the methodology
- "We've helped companies like yours" with no specifics
- "Free consultation" — implies the consultation is the product, which signals the consultant has nothing else
- Slide decks with infinite hexagons / circles / pyramids and no actual numbers
- "AI-powered" appended to literally any noun

## Trust signals — what signals you're worth their time

- **A specific worked example** of how Bout Labs approached *exactly the kind
  of problem they have*, even abstractly — not a generic case study
- **Naming the failure mode**, not just the success — "here's the part most teams get wrong"
- **Numbers and durations early in the conversation** — "15 years, 17 industries, 3 fractional-CTO engagements active right now"
- **A real recommendation from a peer they trust** — referral is the dominant acquisition channel for this persona
- **Restraint** — being willing to say "we're not the right fit for that"
- **The client logo strip** on boutlabs.com works because the names are recognizable and the audience can verify

## CTA they'll act on

This persona doesn't click "Book a demo." They reply to a real human, in
their inbox or a DM, asking for a 30-minute conversation. Or they
introduce themselves at an event.

- **High-conversion CTAs for them**:
    - "Let's talk." (with a contextual paragraph above it that proves you've read their situation)
    - "Here's a 600-word note on how we'd think about your case — happy to walk through it on a call."
    - "Reply if you'd like an intro to a client of ours who's solved this." (delivered via warm intro, not cold)
- **CTAs that don't work**:
    - "BOOK A DEMO", "Sign up for our newsletter", "Download the whitepaper"
    - Any CTA that requires them to fill in a form before getting a human
    - "Let me know if you're interested" (low-affordance — they won't)

## Adjacent personas

- **Internal champions** — VP of Engineering, Director of Product, Head of IT — they're not the buyer but they're the budget influencer and the implementation partner; copy that makes them look smart to the buyer is high-leverage
- **Board members / investors** — often the source of the introduction; content that gets shared peer-to-peer in this layer drives more pipeline than any direct push to the buyer
- **Industry peer founders** — they don't buy from us but they refer; the playbook is "ship content the peer wants to forward"

## Voice notes for this persona

- **They are not engineers.** Code blocks are noise; analogies and
  decision frameworks are signal. If a piece references technology, it's
  to ground a business decision, not to demonstrate technical chops.
- **They want a calm authority figure**, not a peer cheerleader. Pull the
  voice toward Stratechery-clarity, away from Twitter-startup-bro.
- **Time is the scarce resource.** Every paragraph earns its existence;
  every email respects their inbox.
- **They are skeptical of marketing as a category.** The best Bout Labs
  marketing copy doesn't read like marketing. It reads like a note from
  a trusted advisor who happened to think of them today.

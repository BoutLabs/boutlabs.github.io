The same job, different costumes.

Bout Labs is built around one person’s judgment. Here’s where that judgment comes from.

Fifteen years in, Jesse Curry has done the same job in a dozen different costumes: figure out what’s actually true about a hard technical problem, decide what’s worth building, and get the right people building it. The verticals have ranged from Nintendo cartridges to parathyroid diagnostics to enterprise real estate platforms serving millions of renters. The job has stayed the same.

He started as a gameplay programmer at Gorilla Systems, leading development on All Star Cheer Squad (Nintendo DS, published by THQ) and Sam Skye: Rock PI (Nintendo Wii) — an early lesson in shipping on fixed hardware, fixed deadlines, and no patch-Tuesday safety net.

From there, nearly a decade at Haneke Design, where he joined as the company’s first mobile developer in 2010 and helped move it from an interactive design studio into a full software shop — building code review, QA, and CI practices into a team that hadn’t needed them before. The projects show the range on their own. OneWorld Explorer, the iPad experience rented to visitors at the One World Observatory atop One World Trade Center, drew over 100,000 rentals in its first summer alone. Calcium Pro, built for the Norman Parathyroid Center on a dataset of four million data points from 30,000 patients, was rated a top-3 medical app three years running. The Z5 video relay platform for ZVRS gave Deaf and hard-of-hearing users free, FCC-regulated, 24/7 video interpretation into the hearing telephone network — a project where “ship it” meant “make sure this works for someone with no fallback if it doesn’t.”

As VP of Delivery at Blackbird Studios in the San Francisco Bay Area, Jesse ran the process side of a studio that partnered with design firms like IDEO on production engineering — including a team that won a $188M contract against 450-plus competing vendors. The job wasn’t writing the code; it was making sure the code matched what a demanding design partner had promised a client.

As CTO of The Breakaway, Jesse led engineering for an AI-driven cycling-performance platform — members saw a 15% average improvement in ride output and a 10-watt FTP gain in their first 90 days. Strava acquired the technology in 2025.

He studied philosophy at USF before any of this, which explains more about how he works than his resume does — the job has always been figuring out what’s actually true before deciding what to build.

Today he’s a Senior Director of Software Engineering at Greystar, one of the largest real estate management and investment firms in the world, and he’s been Bout Labs’ strategic advisor since 2016 — which is really the same job again: figure out what’s true, decide what’s worth doing, get the right people on it.

The throughline: a hospitality ML pipeline, a parathyroid dataset, a cycling power meter, and a property-management platform look nothing alike. The decisions underneath them are the same handful, made over and over. What’s actually true here. What’s worth building. Who should build it. How you’ll know if it’s going sideways before it gets expensive. That’s the job Bout Labs sells — and it’s the job Jesse’s been doing since before the company existed.
